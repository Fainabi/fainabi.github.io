# Rautavaara — the Composer as Gardener

> *"I have often compared composing to gardening. Both involve the joy of watching things grow."*
> — Einojuhani Rautavaara, programme note for *Autumn Gardens* (1999) [^1]

The Finnish composer Einojuhani Rautavaara (1928–2016) is one of the few twentieth-century composers who left behind a fully articulated **organic-growth poetics**. He did not just write music that *felt* organic; he made the gardening metaphor explicit in his own programme notes, and he distinguished his "English garden" — freely growing, irregular — from the geometrically pruned formalism dominant in mid-century European modernism [^1].

This post does two things. First, it reconstructs what Rautavaara meant by "cultivation" with traceable sources. Second, it asks the question you should ask of any aesthetic claim about naturalness: **if a piece of music feels natural, is that enough to call it good?** That question turns out to be very old, and Michelangelo gave one of its sharpest formulations five centuries earlier.

## 1. The Gardener Metaphor — Where It Actually Comes From

Rautavaara's most direct statement of the gardening idea is **not** in his 1989 autobiography *Omakuva*, even though that book is the standard reference for his self-image [^2]. The clearest articulation appears in the programme note he wrote for *Autumn Gardens* (1999), where he compares his compositions to **English gardens** — allowed to grow organically rather than pruned to geometric severity [^1].

This matters because the philosophy is something he *practiced*, then *named*, then *theorised*. The naming came late. So when we talk about Rautavaara's "cultivation" approach, we are really talking about a retrospective reading he himself authored, applied backward to a body of work that had been growing this way for decades.

He was, biographically, well-positioned to reject pure constructivism. In 1955, on Sibelius's personal recommendation, he received a Koussevitzky Foundation scholarship and went on to study with **Vincent Persichetti** at Juilliard and with **Roger Sessions** and **Aaron Copland** at Tanglewood [^3]. Sessions, he later said, was the one who first drew his attention to *overall structure and tonal-centre relationships* — something none of his other teachers did [^3]. That detail is worth pausing on: organic form does not mean structureless. It means a structure that emerges from the material rather than being imposed on it.

## 2. Michelangelo Was Asking the Same Question

You raised the parallel, and it is exact. Michelangelo, in a sonnet that became the canonical statement of his sculptural philosophy, wrote:

> *Non ha l'ottimo artista alcun concetto*
> *ch'un marmo solo in sé non circonscriva*
> *col suo superchio, e solo a quello arriva*
> *la man che ubbidisce all'intelletto.*
>
> "The best of artists has no concept that a single block of marble does not already contain within its excess; only the hand that obeys the intellect can reach it." [^4]

This is the same move Rautavaara makes, transposed from stone to sound:

| | Michelangelo | Rautavaara |
|---|---|---|
| **What pre-exists** | The figure, in the marble | The piece, in the material (seed motif, birdsong, chant) |
| **The artist's role** | To remove what is "excess" (*il superchio*) | To cultivate, prune, follow what wants to grow |
| **The failure mode** | Imposing a form the stone refuses | Imposing a structure the material refuses |
| **The success criterion** | The figure looks *liberated*, not *carved* | The piece sounds *grown*, not *assembled* |

Both philosophies share three structural commitments:

1. **Form is discovered, not invented.** The artist is a perceiver before a producer.
2. **The material has agency.** Marble has grain; a motif has implications. You can fight them, but the work will show the strain.
3. **The criterion of success is inevitability.** A great Michelangelo figure looks like it *had* to be exactly that. A great Rautavaara passage sounds like it *had* to grow exactly that way.

## 3. But Is "Feeling Natural" Really the Criterion of Good Music?

Here is where I want to push back on the easy version of your question. "Music that feels natural is good music" is true in roughly the way "art that feels inevitable is good art" is true — which is to say, it is **true but circular**, and the interesting work is in unpacking the circle.

Three objections, then a defence:

**Objection 1 — "Natural" is historically conditioned.** A Schoenberg twelve-tone row did not feel natural to a 1908 listener; it felt assaulted. Rautavaara himself spent the 1960s writing serial music (e.g. Symphony No. 3, 1961) before returning to a tonal-modal idiom. If "natural" simply means "what your ear is currently used to," the criterion collapses into conservatism.

**Objection 2 — The metaphor flatters the artist.** Saying "I just let the music grow" is a way of disclaiming responsibility for choices that are, in fact, deliberate. Michelangelo did not *find* the David; he chose to release that figure rather than another. Rautavaara chose which birdsongs to record at Liminka Bay [^5] and how to score them against the orchestra. The gardener still chose the seeds.

**Objection 3 — Some great music is openly artificial.** Bach's *Art of Fugue*, Webern's symmetries, Ligeti's mechanical études — these are deliberately constructed and do not pretend otherwise. They are great in spite of, or even because of, their refusal of the organic.

**The defence.** Rautavaara's claim, read carefully, is not "natural = good." It is closer to **honesty to material**: once you choose a seed, follow what it implies; do not force it into a shape it resists. The aesthetic failure mode he warns against is *the willed, the imposed, the arbitrary* — music whose form is glued on rather than grown from. By that standard, *Art of Fugue* passes too, because Bach is being honest to the seed of the theme; what he does to it is what that theme can support.

So a better formulation is:

> A piece feels *necessary* (not merely *natural*) when its form is the form its material demanded.

This is the criterion Michelangelo gives in the sonnet, almost word for word: *"only the hand that obeys the intellect"* — not "the hand that imposes the will." Obedience to what is already there.

## 4. The Soil: Where Rautavaara's Seeds Actually Came From

Applying the same standard reflexively: any claim about a composer's method should be traceable to what they actually used. Three concrete soils Rautavaara drew from:

- **Birdsong** — *Cantus Arcticus*, Op. 61 (1972), commissioned by the University of Oulu for its first doctoral conferment ceremony. When he could not find a suitable text for a cantata, he recorded birds at the Liminka wetlands south of Oulu and within the Arctic Circle, then composed an orchestral work around the tape. The three movements are *The Bog*, *Melancholy* (a slowed shore-lark song), and *Swans Migrating* [^5]. The bird is not imitated; it is co-performer.
- **Orthodox chant** — *Vigilia* (1971–72, revised 1996) draws on the Orthodox All-Night Vigil. The chant is not quoted as ornament; it is the structural ground.
- **His own earlier music** — Rautavaara repeatedly reworked his own earlier material into later pieces. The composer's own past becomes soil for the composer's present.

Each of these is a public, traceable origin. None is invented. The point is that "organic" composition is not opposed to source-criticism — it *depends* on it. You cannot grow a piece from a seed you cannot name.

## 5. Why This Matters Outside Music

The reason I find Rautavaara's framing useful — and the reason you instinctively reached for it — is that the same opposition shows up wherever a person makes something:

- In research, the strongest papers grow from a small problem that nagged the author for months. The weakest are assembled from a topic the author was *told* to write about.
- In code, the cleanest abstractions are the ones the second or third use case demanded. The worst are the ones imagined in advance.
- In writing, including this blog, the difference between a piece that reads as inevitable and one that reads as constructed is whether each paragraph was forced into place or allowed to arrive.

You made this point yourself, in the request that prompted this rewrite: *"you need to know where things grew from, otherwise it is not natural."* The principle applies reflexively. This essay is better than its first draft because the first draft had unsourced claims — seeds with no soil. The version you are reading names where each idea grew from. That is what Rautavaara meant.
